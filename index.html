I make retail AI useful for the people who actually use it.

Most of my work sits inside PromoSmart, the AI-native promo optimization tool inside Impact Analytics' platform. The users are category managers, marketing leads, and super users at global retailers — people who plan hundreds of promotions a month and used to do most of that planning in Excel. My job is to make the tool fast enough, legible enough, and trustworthy enough that they don't have to.

Before Impact Analytics I interned at Lythouse. I work closely with product, engineering, and data science — most of what I ship is co-designed with one or more of them.

Case Study A · Step 3 Discount Screen
47 min → 12 min
The screen that kept planners running a parallel Excel — and the four iterations it took to fix it.
7 min read·Lead designer ·12 weeks·2024
"Step 3 is the main problem."

That was the PM's verdict in our May review. One sentence. He didn't soften it.

He was right to be blunt. Step 3 was where a category manager at our largest customer finalised ~300 promos a month, across 10,000+ SKU-store rows. PostHog said they spent 47 minutes per promo there — most of it scrolling. CSM said it was already showing up in two enterprise renewal conversations as a friction point.

If I couldn't fix Step 3 in three months, we were heading into Q2 renewals naked.

Role. Lead designer (Senior UX UI Designer)  ·  Team. 1 frontend engineer + 1 data scientist  ·  Duration. 12 weeks  ·  Scale. Tenant datasets up to 200,000 rows

ResearchWhat the data said — four problems

Problem 01
Finding what matters
Planners couldn't tell which of 10,000+ rows needed their attention.
"I scroll for 20 minutes and still don't know if I missed a SKU." — Apparel category manager (CSM interview)
PostHog · 14 undo events per session.
Problem 02
Time grain
Planners needed weekly / daily views; the table only showed promo-period summary.
"If I want to see it per week, how do I do it?" — PM, May review
PostHog · 8.3 Excel-switch events per session (count of times a planner alt-tabbed from PromoSmart to Excel during a single Step 3 session).
Problem 03
Aggregation
The same screen had to serve three personas thinking at three altitudes — category, item, item × store.
"Some weeks I plan the whole shirt category. Some weeks just one SKU."
Problem 04
Analysis outside the tool
Planners did the analysis in Excel pivots, then came back to PromoSmart just to enter numbers.
"Excel pivots tell me what's broken in 30 seconds. Your table tells me nothing."

Prior artLandscape scan

Before designing, I scanned how five competitors handle dense planning grids — Oracle Retail (ORC), Revionics, Blue Yonder, Cognira, Eversight. Pattern: all of them pushed planners through 50–500-column tables with no exception-driven attention. None had a time-grain drawer.

Internal precedent. MarkSmart's exception-alert pattern was the closest pre-existing IA-internal solution. PriceSmart had a similar approach.

Cross-industry inspiration. Notion's database views, Airtable's grouping, Linear's filter chips, Stripe's data tables, and Excel pivot tables.

North starSuccess metric I chose

Excel-switch count per Step 3 session (PostHog). If planners stopped opening Excel, every other problem was downstream. Target: under 2 (from 8.3).

DesignIterations

01 Top-of-table summary chart Killed Tackled Problem 1

Why. PM said tables alone weren't intuitive. Hypothesis: category-level context up top would frame row-level decisions.

Alternatives considered
  • Inline mini-charts per row — pattern from Stripe and Linear; deferred.
  • Chart-as-modal overlay on demand — pattern from Tableau Server; rejected because it hid the table.
Chosen approach. Single line chart pinned to top of table. Drew on Cognira's layout pattern.

Result. PostHog showed planners glanced at the chart for under 2 seconds. CSM: "It's nice but it doesn't help me decide on row 47."

The dominant industry pattern was wrong for our problem. Decisions happen at the row, not the category.

02 Alert chips on high-priority rows Won Tackled Problem 1

Why. 10,000 rows can't be scanned. We needed exception-driven attention. After Iter 1 failed, we ran an internal sketch workshop (DS + design + PM, 90 min) — three alternatives below came out of it.

Alternatives considered
  • Sortable columns by variance / revenue — pattern from Eversight and Blue Yonder; rejected because G2 reviews of both listed "can't tell which column to sort by" as a top complaint.
  • Heat-map colouring across whole rows — pattern from Tableau and ThoughtSpot; rejected — perf at 10K rows, and heat maps are ambient (chips are focal).
  • Separate "Needs attention" side panel — pattern from Salesforce sidebar; rejected — action and signal need to coexist in one view.
Chosen approach. Coloured chips inline on rows, with a sticky "Alerts only" filter pill. Adapted from MarkSmart's exception-highlight pattern, reinforced by a planner's own suggestion: "color the rows that are weird so I don't miss them."
How / collaboration

Worked with the DS team to define exception rules — forecast variance > 20%, top 5% of SKUs by revenue, items below margin floor, new SKUs with no baseline. Frontend eng flagged a perf risk: rendering 10,000 chips would tank scroll. We agreed to virtualize the table.

Result. Planners opened "Alerts only" in 71% of sessions. Excel-switch count dropped 8.3 → 4.6.

03 Time-grain drawer Won Tackled Problems 2 + 4

Why. Problem 2 said planners wanted weekly / daily views; Problem 4 said they did this work in Excel pivots. A single drawer could solve both — if it was as fast as Excel.

Alternatives considered
  • Inline expansion of each row — pattern from Notion's row expansion and GitHub PR diffs; rejected because only one row is visible at a time.
  • Full-screen weekly view (modal) — pattern from Tableau drill-through; rejected — hides the table.
  • Pop-out window — pattern from Salesforce Lightning; rejected — second window creates a second context.
Chosen approach. Side drawer at 60/40 split. Drew on Linear's issue side panel and Figma's properties panel.
How / collaboration

Eng pushed back on 60/40 — proposed 70/30. Prototyped both with 6 planners; 60/40 won. DS partnership: materialized weekly forecast table refreshed nightly — traded freshness for sub-400ms drawer load.

Result. Excel-switch count dropped 4.6 → 1.4. CSM verbatim: "I don't open Excel anymore for Step 3."

04 Pivot / aggregation toggle Won Tackled Problem 3

Why. Three personas, three grains. A single screen with grain control was simpler than forking the codebase.

Alternatives considered
  • Three separate screens (one per grain) — pattern from Oracle ORC; rejected — tripled the codebase.
  • Saved views with pre-filtered aggregations — pattern from Salesforce reports and Looker explores; rejected — requires foreknowledge.
  • Group-by dropdown (Excel-style pivot) — pattern from Excel pivot tables and Airtable grouping; rejected — too many degrees of freedom.
Chosen approach. Breadcrumb-style grain selector — Category → Sub-category → Item → Item × Store. Adapted from Notion's nested page navigation and Stripe's drill-through breadcrumbs.

Result. Promos finalised per planner per week went 11 → 28.

Cross-functionalEngineering & data science partnership

Perf set the design ceiling. Frontend eng flagged early that per-row visual treatment needed virtualization to keep 60fps scrolling on 10K rows.

The DS team owned the model; I owned the surface. We co-designed an exception-rule engine where DS controlled the math and I controlled the discoverability. The rules changed three times in the first three months — separation let the model evolve without UI rework.

I sequenced the launch with engineering. Iter 2 (alerts) → sprint 1; Iter 3 (drawer) → sprints 2–3; Iter 4 (pivot) → sprints 4–5. Planners got value early instead of waiting for one big launch.

What I'd do differently

We defined the exception rules with the DS team and one apparel super user. Six weeks post-launch, grocery planners told CSM the rules were wrong — they think about exceptions differently (perishability, promo timing, store density). We patched it with configurable rules in v1.1. Should have been v1, and I knew it during research. I traded correctness for speed and paid for it twice.

Case Study B · AI Trust & Explainability
23% → 71%
Acceptance rate on our AI's promo recommendations. Four design moves got us there — none of them was the AI getting smarter.
7 min read·Lead designer ·6 weeks·2024
"It gives me a number. I don't know if that number is good."

That was an apparel merchant in interview number three. By interview number ten I'd heard variations of the same sentence from every planner.

We had built a smarter discount engine than most of our customers had ever used. PostHog said they ignored it 77% of the time. "Copy Last Year" — the anti-pattern we'd literally built the AI to kill — was still being used on 54% of new promos. Two of three lost deals in Q1 cited "AI feels like a black box" in post-mortems.

The AI didn't need to get smarter. It needed to be trusted. I had six weeks.

Role. Lead designer (Senior UX UI Designer)  ·  Team. 1 data scientist + 1 frontend engineer  ·  Duration. 6 weeks

ResearchWhat the data said — four problems

Problem 01
Precision
A single number reads as a guess.
"It gives me a number. I don't know if that number is good."
PostHog · AI acceptance = 23%.
Problem 02
Reasoning
Even a precise number is rejected without a "why."
"Show me the math, or I'm not signing my name to this discount."
Support tickets tagged "AI logic question" = 47/month.
Problem 03
Comparison
Planners think comparatively, not absolutely.
"What if I gave 5% more?"
"Copy Last Year" usage = 54% of new promos.
Problem 04
Constraints
Trust requires knowing the AI won't get you into trouble.
"What if I'm 5% over budget? Will the system tell me?"
CSM trust survey baseline — 31% agreed "I trust the AI recommendation."

Prior artLandscape scan

AI explainability is moving fast across SaaS — uniformly in the wrong direction for our users.

Competitors. Eversight had the strongest AI-suggests pattern but no inline reasoning. Revionics displayed confidence scores but no "why." Blue Yonder's AI was effectively opaque. Cognira was newer and more transparent but still surfaced reasoning behind a separate click.

Cross-industry. SHAP-style feature attribution is standard in ML tooling (Hugging Face, Pinecone, OpenAI's playground) — technically correct, unreadable for our users. Linear's AI commit summaries, Notion AI's inline suggestions, and GitHub Copilot all use natural language.

Internal precedent. MarkSmart had started a similar pattern. We extended it.

North starSuccess metric I chose

AI recommendation acceptance rate (PostHog event). Target: over 60%. Secondary floor: "Copy Last Year" under 25%.

DesignIterations

01Confidence band on the recommendationPartial winTackled Problem 1

Why. A precise single number reads as a guess; a range reads as analysis.

Alternatives considered
  • Hide the AI's number until the planner enters their own — pattern from Zillow Zestimate; rejected — removing the recommendation defeats the purpose.
  • Number + confidence percentage — pattern from weather forecasts; rejected — "87% confident in what?" tested confusingly.
Chosen approach. Shaded confidence band. Drew on Tableau's forecast widgets and Stripe's churn dashboards.

Result. Acceptance 23% → 31%. Modest. CSM: "Now I want to know why."

Confidence framing is the floor of trust, not the ceiling.

02"Why this discount?" panelWonTackled Problem 2

Why. PM literally asked for it — "GPT which is best and why it was chosen."

Alternatives considered
  • Always-visible expanded reasoning — pattern from Hugging Face's transformer demos; rejected — ate vertical space.
  • Chatbot-style "ask why" interaction — pattern from Notion AI and ChatGPT plugins; rejected — too open-ended.
  • SHAP-style feature attribution chart — standard in ML tooling; rejected — technically correct, unreadable.
Chosen approach. Four reasoning cards — elasticity, similar SKU history, budget remaining, expected lift — each one line of plain English. Drew on GitHub Copilot's inline suggestions and Linear's AI commit messages.
How / collaboration

Designed the copy with the DS team — three rounds on the elasticity card alone, from "highly price-elastic" (too vague) to the final "shirts lift ~3.4% per 1% discount up to 35%."

Result. Panel opened on 64% of recommendations. Acceptance 31% → 58%. Support tickets 47/mo → 22/mo.

Plain-English reasoning beats SHAP. Every time.

03Scenario comparison — the Copy-Last-Year killerWonTackled Problem 3

Why. Planners decide comparatively. "Copy Last Year" was being used because it was the only comparison the product offered.

Alternatives considered
  • Single AI suggestion + manual "try another number" — pattern from Oracle ORC; rejected — forced planner to do the math.
  • Slider that re-forecasts in real-time — pattern from interactive ML demos / Streamlit apps; rejected — forecast model took 800ms+ per scenario.
  • 5-scenario fan — rejected — cognitive load too high.
Chosen approach. Triptych — three discount scenarios side-by-side. AI's pick highlighted. Drew on Airbnb's pricing tools and Notion's pricing layouts.

Result. "Copy Last Year" 54% → 24%. Acceptance 58% → 68%. Scenario view used in 81% of new offers.

04Budget-aware AI (the guardrail)WonTackled Problem 4

Why. PM's framing — "40% you are selling 4000, but it is within your budget."

Alternatives considered
  • Hide out-of-budget scenarios — pattern from Mint; rejected — planners need to know what's possible.
  • Hard-block out-of-budget submissions — pattern from SAP Ariba; rejected — warning > block.
  • Budget tracker as a separate page — pattern from NetSuite; rejected — trust comes from constraints visible at decision time.
Chosen approach. AI constrained to budget; persistent budget bar; out-of-budget scenarios flagged with a warning chip. Drew on GitHub's diff warnings and Linear's workspace-limit warnings.

Result. Acceptance 68% → 71%. Trust survey (n=58): 31% → 78% agreed "I trust the AI recommendation."

Cross-functionalEngineering & data science partnership

Co-ownership of model output language. Every reasoning card in Iter 2 went through joint design-DS review. This separates "we shipped explainability" from "the explainability earned trust."

Engineering made trust feel instant. Pre-fetched scenarios in Iter 3 and the live-updating budget bar in Iter 4 only worked because of frontend perf decisions.

What I'd do differently

I over-indexed on "presentation of uncertainty" because it was the easier change. The right call was to ship reasoning first. I knew it from the research and I sequenced wrong anyway.

Case Study C · Decision Dashboard
4.7 → 1.1 / week
Excel exports per planner. Our biggest customer was paying us to be ignored — until they deleted the spreadsheet.
7 min read·Lead designer ·8 weeks·2024
"We're a system of input, not a system of record."

That was the PM's framing the week we started this project. It's the kind of thing you don't want said about your enterprise SaaS — because the next sentence is usually about churn.

He was being polite. The truth was uglier: our largest customer's super users had built their own parallel Excel and were running the entire season on it. They were paying Impact Analytics roughly $400K a year to be a glorified data-export button.

If the dashboard couldn't replace that Excel, the renewal conversation in eight weeks was going to be about price, not value.

Role. Lead designer (Senior UX UI Designer)  ·  Team. 2 frontend engineers + 1 data engineer  ·  Duration. 8 weeks

ResearchWhat the data said — four problems

Problem 01
Wrong question framing
Dashboard answered "what's the data?"; planners asked "what's broken?"
"I export because I can't see what's broken from your dashboard."
PostHog · time-to-find-variance = 19 minutes.
Problem 02
Misdiagnosed cause
Stakeholders kept saying "no charts." It wasn't.
"Why is everything a table? I just want one chart." — same super user, two interviews later: "You added charts. I still export to Excel."
Problem 03
One dashboard for three personas
Super users, marketing leads, and merchants saw the wrong information first.
"I open the dashboard. The first thing I see is revenue. I don't care about revenue. I care about which event is breaking."
PostHog · filter-setup events per session = 4.8.
Problem 04
Excel-to-rows drill speed
Excel let planners go from "weird number" to "which rows caused it" in one click. We made them do five.
"In Excel I see a number, I click, I'm in the rows. In your tool I have to remember the filters."

Prior artLandscape scan

The dashboard space splits between two camps — neither fit our user.

Analyst tools. Tableau, Looker, Sigma, ThoughtSpot, Hex: powerful exploration, demand SQL-adjacent literacy.

Planner tools. Cognira, Anaplan, Symphony RetailAI, Aptos: assume fixed workflow.

Internal precedent. MarkSmart's variance-first reporting; PriceSmart's exception-driven dashboards.

Cross-industry. Linear's Triage view, Stripe's dashboard, Datadog and Honeycomb, and the dominant real competitor — Excel pivot tables.

North starSuccess metric I chose

Excel-export count per planner per week (PostHog). Target: under 1.5 (from 4.7).

DesignIterations

01Table → chart toggleKilledTackled Problem 2

Why. Stakeholders and PM said charts were missing. Easiest possible change.

Alternatives considered
  • Mandatory chart-only view — pattern from Tableau dashboards; rejected as too aggressive.
  • Charts pinned above tables — pattern from Cognira and Eversight; rejected for the same reason as Case A's Iter 1.
Chosen approach. Per-component table / chart toggle. Drew on Looker's view-switcher.

Result. Toggle clicked on 18% of sessions, then abandoned. CSM: "Now it's a chart. I still don't know what to look at."

Adding chart-shaped data to a dashboard built around the wrong question doesn't help. The format wasn't the bottleneck; the question framing was.

02Variance-first dashboardWonTackled Problem 1

Why. Planners' actual question is "where am I off forecast?" — not "what's my revenue?"

Alternatives considered
  • Configurable home dashboard — pattern from Tableau and Looker; rejected — most users never customize.
  • AI-generated daily summary — pattern from Slack AI and Notion AI digests; deferred.
  • Anomaly detection alerts — pattern from Datadog, Honeycomb, PagerDuty; rejected for v1 — insufficient training data.
Chosen approach. "Top variance today" default view. Drew on Linear's Triage view and MarkSmart's variance reporting.

Result. Time-to-find-variance 19 min → 3 min by week 4. Drill-downs 2.1 → 4.4.

Lead with the user's question, not the data.

03Persona-based defaultsWonTackled Problem 3

Why. PM defined three personas. A single default can't serve three.

Alternatives considered
  • Manual "set my default view" — pattern from Salesforce dashboards; rejected — most never configure.
  • AI-personalised dashboard — pattern from Slack's "For You" and LinkedIn's home; rejected — would conflict with the AI trust work in Case B.
  • Smart-section reordering by role — too clever for v1.
Chosen approach. Three role-tagged default views. Drew on Notion's workspace-per-role pattern and Asana's "My Tasks".

Result. Filter-setup events dropped 64%. Dashboard DAU 38% → 76%.

Defaults matter more than configurability. Most users never customise.

04Drill-from-chart to source rowsWonTackled Problem 4

Why. Excel got used because of one specific thing — when planners spotted a weird number, they wanted to see the rows behind it immediately.

Alternatives considered
  • "View details" button per chart — pattern from Looker and Sigma; rejected — still a separate click.
  • Hover-preview of underlying rows — pattern from Notion's hover-cards and Linear's issue previews; rejected — too noisy.
  • Linked filter pills — pattern from Tableau actions; kept partially.
Chosen approach. Every chart point clickable. Drew on Stripe's drill-from-anywhere and Excel's native drill-through.

Result. Excel-export count 4.7 → 1.1 / planner / week. 9 of 14 power users at the lead account stopped maintaining a side Excel.

Cross-functionalEngineering & data science partnership

The data engineer's materialized variance table was the unlock. Iter 2 couldn't have shipped without it.

Sub-600ms drill was the trust unlock. The Excel-killing argument fell apart if our drill felt slow.

I deliberately deferred two AI features because the AI trust work in Case B was still landing in parallel. Strategic call I made with the PM — design judgment across products.

What I'd do differently
